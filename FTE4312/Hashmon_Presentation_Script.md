# Hashmon Presentation Script

## Cover
Hello everyone. I am Baohua Fang, student number 123090108. Today I will present Hashmon, a user-generated, cross-game digital companion ecosystem.

## Slide 1 — Outline
In this presentation, I will briefly introduce the motivation, the system design, the working prototype, and the final results of the project.

## Slide 2 — Personal Motivation
This idea came from a personal feeling. When I played Pokémon, I became attached to my Eevee as if it were a real companion. But once that game ended, I could not bring it into another game and continue the adventure. That sense of loss is exactly what inspired Hashmon.

## Slide 3 — The Problem
More broadly, many Pokémon players share the same feeling. They invest time, emotion, and creativity into digital companions, but traditional games lock those companions inside closed systems. Ownership and continuity are missing.

## Slide 4 — The Goal
That is why Hashmon was designed. The goal of Hashmon is controlled interoperability. I want to show that one NFT companion can preserve its identity and still remain meaningful across different gameplay contexts.

## Slide 5 — System Architecture
To achieve this goal, Hashmon is structured in the following way. Phaser runs the game client, MetaMask and Ethers.js handle wallet interaction, Sepolia hosts the NFT contracts, and IPFS stores the metadata and artwork.

## Slide 6 — Workflow
With this architecture, the user flow is complete and easy to follow. The player creates a Hashmon, uploads metadata, mints the NFT, syncs it to the wallet, and then reuses it in gameplay scenes and in the marketplace.

## Slide 7 — Prototype Evidence
This slide shows that the prototype really works. The player can customize the companion, mint it on-chain, and immediately see it appear inside the wallet-linked inventory.

## Slide 8 — Interoperability Proof
If the project stopped there, it would be just another Web3 game demo. However, here comes the key twist. The same NFT companion, or Hashmon, can appear in both the battle and garden scenes. Its identity is preserved, but each scene interprets the same Hashmon differently. For example, in the battle scene, the stats affect combat performance, while in the garden scene, they influence mood and interaction.

## Slide 9 — Marketplace and Ownership Loop
In addition, Hashmon also supports a marketplace flow. Users can list, browse, and buy Hashmons on Sepolia. This gives the NFT both gameplay value and exchange value.

## Slide 10 — Results and Current Limits
The prototype already demonstrates wallet connection, NFT minting, IPFS storage, marketplace flow, and cross-scene reuse. At the same time, it is still a prototype and can be extended with deeper gameplay and broader interoperability standards.

## Slide 11 — Conclusion
To conclude, Hashmon demonstrates that a player-created companion can become a persistent digital asset with verifiable ownership and controlled reuse across multiple gameplay contexts.
